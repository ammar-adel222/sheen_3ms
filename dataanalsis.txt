Data spans 10+ sectors — your model MUST generalize across all of them, not overfit to restaurant Arabic. 
Here's what I found after thoroughly analyzing both files. Let me walk you through the key thinking before implementation.
What the data actually is
You have 1,971 training reviews and 500 validation reviews, spanning 10+ sectors (restaurants, ecommerce, transport, healthcare, beauty, etc.) from two platforms — Google Maps and Play Store. Labels are perfectly consistent with zero mismatches, which is a gift.
The 5 decisions that will make or break your F1-micro score
1. Don't strip newlines. 18% of reviews use \n to separate aspects. That structure is signal — each line often maps to a distinct aspect. Encode newlines as a [NL] token rather than collapsing them.
2. Don't strip emojis. A review saying جيد😔😻 is labeled negative despite جيد meaning "good" — the sad emoji overrides the word. Map emojis to sentiment tokens before processing.
3. Address the neutral class immediately. Only 4.5% of sentiment labels are neutral (149 out of 3,333). Without class-weighted loss (approximately 5× weight on neutral) or focal loss, your model will never predict neutral. This is the most common silent killer of F1-micro in imbalanced ABSA.
4. Use a dialectal Arabic model. The data mixes Egyptian, Gulf, Levantine, and MSA Arabic, plus some English code-switching. AraBERT v2 or CAMeL-BERT-Mix are the right choices. Standard Arabic NLP tools (Farasa, MADAMIRA) assume MSA and will fail on the dialect content.
5. Inject platform and business_category as features. Play Store reviews → app_experience is almost guaranteed. Google Maps → physical venue aspects. These priors are free signal for aspect detection.
